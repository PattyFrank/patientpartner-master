The $22 Billion Engagement Crisis

Pharma invests more in patient support than ever before. The returns have never been worse.

According to research from Phreesia, GoodRx, and IQVIA, the industry allocates over $22 billion annually to patient support programs—hub services, copay assistance, nurse educators, digital tools, call centers. Yet only 3–8% of eligible patients actually use these programs. And 59% of patients report little or no knowledge that support programs even exist.

The problem isn't a lack of programs. It's a fundamental misalignment between what programs deliver and what patients need at the moment of decision.

What Programs Deliver vs. What Patients Need

What PSPs Typically Provide

  • Prior authorization support
  • Copay cards and financial assistance
  • Nurse educator phone calls
  • Automated refill reminders (SMS/email)
  • Specialty pharmacy coordination
  • Enrollment in hub services

What Patients Actually Need

  • Someone who's been through the same treatment
  • Confidence the treatment is worth the fear
  • A real conversation with a real patient
  • Answers to questions they won't ask their doctor
  • Reassurance during the hardest moments
  • Proof that life gets better on the other side

This is what we call the "missing middle"—the psychological gap between enrollment and first fill that no amount of logistics can close. A patient can have their prior auth approved, their copay covered, and their specialty pharmacy on standby. They still won't start therapy if they're afraid.

Why Peer Mentorship Outperforms

Three structural advantages explain the performance gap:

1. Emotional resonance beats informational content. A nurse educator can explain side effects. A mentor who experienced them can say "Here's what the first week actually felt like, and here's why it was worth it." That distinction drives action.

2. On-demand timing matches decision windows. Traditional programs schedule follow-ups weeks out. Patients make treatment decisions in moments of fear and uncertainty—at 10pm, after reading side effects online. Real-time peer access meets patients when the decision is actually happening.

3. Matched experience creates instant credibility. Generic communities require patients to sift through hundreds of posts. One-to-one matching by condition, treatment, demographics, and experience creates an immediate connection: "This person understands exactly what I'm going through."

Chapter 4

The 5-Principle Framework for Effective Peer-to-Peer Programs

Not all peer programs are created equal. Patient ambassador panels, online forums, and casual support groups have existed for years. The programs that produce measurable clinical and commercial outcomes share five principles.

The P2P Mentorship Framework

1

Precision Matching, Not Random Assignment

Match mentors to patients by condition, treatment, demographics, and lived experience. A 55-year-old woman starting biologics for RA needs a mentor who completed that specific therapy—not a general "patient advocate." The data shows matched mentorship produces 3–5x the connection rate of unmatched programs (73% vs. 10–20%).

2

Real-Time Access, Not Scheduled Callbacks

Treatment decisions happen in moments, not business hours. Programs that offer same-day or on-demand peer connections capture the decision window. Programs that schedule calls two weeks out lose patients to fear, doubt, and inertia. The connection must be available when the patient is ready, not when the coordinator has an opening.

3

Trained Mentors, Not Unstructured Volunteers

Effective mentors are vetted, trained, and supported. They know how to share their experience without giving medical advice. They know when to encourage, when to listen, and when to refer. In-house programs struggle here because training and managing a mentor pool is operationally intensive. Dedicated platforms handle mentor recruitment, training, compliance monitoring, and quality assurance.

4

Measurable Outcomes, Not Engagement Theater

Track what matters: treatment starts, adherence at 30/60/90/180 days, days on therapy, progression in care journey. If your peer program measures "calls completed" or "sessions scheduled" but can't tie those to commercial outcomes, it's not delivering. Real programs capture sentiment data, decision progression, and downstream treatment behavior.

5

Enterprise Compliance, Not Workarounds

HIPAA, SOC 2, ISO 27001, adverse event reporting, and audit trails aren't optional in pharma. Every patient interaction must be logged, monitored, and accessible to compliance teams. Programs that rely on phone calls, social media groups, or unmonitored community platforms create regulatory exposure. Compliance must be built into the infrastructure, not bolted on.

Key Decisions at Each Phase

Discovery: Where Are You Losing Patients?

Use your existing data to identify the highest-impact drop-off points. For most programs, the biggest gap is between enrollment and first fill (the "missing middle"). But some brands see significant attrition at 90 days, during treatment switching, or at the transition from clinical trial to commercial product. The peer program should target the moment with the highest revenue impact.

Build: In-House vs. Platform

Some companies attempt to build peer programs internally. Takeda's "My Mentor Connections" for multiple myeloma, for example, enrolled 270 patients over its lifetime—demonstrating the concept works but also showing the scale limitations of in-house approaches. Dedicated platforms can deploy across multiple brands and indications simultaneously, with existing mentor pools (500+ mentors across 40,000+ users in the case of the largest platforms) and proven operational infrastructure.

Pilot: What to Measure

During pilot, track these KPIs weekly:

  • Connection rate—% of patients who connect with a mentor (benchmark: 73%)
  • Decision impact—% who report mentorship influenced their treatment decision (benchmark: 71%)
  • Progression rate—% who take a next step (referral, appointment, Rx) (benchmark: 68%)
  • Patient sentiment—confidence scores pre- and post-connection (benchmark: 90% boost)
  • Time to connection—how quickly patients are matched (target: same day)

Scale: Multi-Brand Expansion

Once the pilot validates outcomes for a single brand or indication, the program architecture should support expansion to additional therapy areas without rebuilding infrastructure. Each new indication requires: new mentor recruitment and training, updated matching criteria, and brand-specific compliance review—but the core platform, operational playbook, and measurement framework carry over.
