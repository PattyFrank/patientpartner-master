# The Patient Drop-Off Scorecard

## Find Where Your Patients Disappear Between Enrollment and First Fill

Pharma invests $22B+ annually in patient support programs. Only 3–8% of eligible patients use them.

That's not a marketing problem. It's a program design problem — and the gap is almost always in the same place.

This scorecard helps you find it. Score your program across 7 dimensions in 5 minutes, identify exactly where patients are falling out of your funnel, and see which gaps are costing you the most starts, adherence, and revenue.

No fluff. No theory. Just a diagnostic you can take to your next leadership meeting with a clear picture of what's working, what's not, and where to focus.

---

## How to Use This Scorecard

**For each statement below, rate your program honestly from 1 to 5:**

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| **1** | We don't do this at all |
| **2** | We've tried, but it's inconsistent or broken |
| **3** | We do this, but it could be better |
| **4** | We do this well and have data to prove it |
| **5** | This is a genuine strength — measurable and repeatable |

Write your score next to each item. Add up each section. Add up your total at the end.

**Tip:** Have 2–3 people on your team score independently, then compare. The gaps between scores are often more revealing than the scores themselves.

## What Your Score Means

### 140–175: Best-in-Class

Your program addresses the full patient journey — logistics, emotional readiness, peer connection, and measurable outcomes. You're in the top tier. Focus on optimization: refining targeting, deepening analytics, and expanding across indications. The data you're collecting is a competitive moat.

**Your move:** Benchmark against your own historical data. Where has improvement plateaued? That's your next unlock.

### 105–139: Strong Foundation, Visible Gaps

You have solid operational infrastructure, but there are specific areas where patients are slipping through. Most programs in this range handle logistics well (prior auth, copay, enrollment) but score low on Sections 3 and 4 — emotional readiness and peer connection. This is the "missing middle": patients who have everything they need to start therapy *except confidence*.

**Your move:** Look at your two lowest-scoring sections. Those aren't just weak spots — they're where your lost patients are. A 10-point improvement in those sections correlates with measurable gains in treatment starts and adherence.

### 70–104: Functional but Leaking

Your program exists, but it's losing patients at multiple points. Enrollment may be slow, post-enrollment support may be thin, and measurement is likely focused on activity (calls, emails) rather than outcomes (starts, adherence, days on therapy). Leadership may be questioning ROI because the program can't prove it.

**Your move:** Prioritize ruthlessly. Don't try to fix everything. Pick the two sections with the lowest scores and focus there. If Section 3 (Emotional Readiness) and Section 4 (Peer Connection) are both below 15, that's your biggest single opportunity.

### Below 70: Rebuilding Opportunity

Your program has significant structural gaps. This isn't a criticism — it's a common starting point, especially for organizations early in their patient support journey or managing legacy systems. The opportunity here is substantial: even moderate improvements from this baseline can produce outsized results.

**Your move:** Start with Sections 1 and 2 (Awareness and Enrollment). You need patients in the funnel before you can support them. Then address Section 3 (Emotional Readiness) — it's the most under-invested area in the industry and the one with the highest return.

## The Pattern We See in the Data

After analyzing thousands of patient interactions, a pattern emerges:

**Most pharma patient support programs score well in Sections 1, 2, 5, and 7** — awareness, enrollment, adherence monitoring, and compliance. These are the operational pillars. Hubs and PSP vendors have invested heavily here for years.

**Most programs score poorly in Sections 3 and 4** — emotional readiness and peer connection. This is the "missing middle."

Here's why that matters:

| What Patients Get | What Patients Need |
|---|---|
| Prior authorization support | Someone who's been through it |
| Copay assistance | Confidence that treatment is worth the fear |
| Nurse educator calls | A real conversation with a real patient |
| Automated refill reminders | Reassurance during the hardest moments |

The industry spends billions on the left column. The right column is where decisions actually happen.

**The data backs this up:**

- Patients who connect with a peer mentor are **68% more likely** to take the next step in their treatment journey (request referral, schedule procedure, fill prescription).
- **71% of patients** report that mentorship was the critical factor in their decision to initiate or continue treatment.
- Programs with real-time peer matching achieve a **73% connection rate** — compared to 10–20% for traditional patient support outreach.
- Peer-supported patients show a **90% increase in confidence** in treatment decisions.
- Patients in mentorship programs remain on therapy an average of **133.5 more days** compared to unsupported patients.

These aren't marginal gains. They're the difference between a patient who starts and stays — and one who disappears.

## How to Use This Scorecard With Your Team

### For your next leadership meeting:

1. **Score independently.** Have 3–5 stakeholders (patient services, commercial, marketing, medical) each complete the scorecard without comparing answers.

2. **Compare section scores.** Where do people agree? Where do they disagree? Disagreements often reveal blind spots — one team thinks a problem is solved, another knows it isn't.

3. **Identify the 2 weakest sections.** These are your highest-ROI investment areas. Present them with the framing: "This is where we're losing the most patients — and here's what the data says about fixing it."

4. **Set a 90-day target.** Pick 3–5 specific items within your weakest sections and define what a 1-point improvement would look like. Make it measurable.

5. **Re-score in 90 days.** Track progress. Share results. Use the scorecard as a recurring diagnostic, not a one-time exercise.

### For building the business case:

If Sections 3 and 4 are your weakest areas, the business case is straightforward:

- **The problem:** Patients enroll but don't start. Your program handles logistics well but doesn't address the psychological barriers — fear, doubt, lack of relatable support — that cause patients to stall.

- **The cost:** Every patient who enrolls but doesn't start represents lost therapy revenue, wasted acquisition spend, and reduced program ROI. For specialty therapies, a single patient's annual therapy value often exceeds $50,000.

- **The solution:** Peer-to-peer mentorship — connecting hesitant patients with trained mentors who've completed the same treatment. The evidence shows 68% more treatment starts, 29% better adherence, and 133.5 more days on therapy.

- **The math:** If your program enrolls 1,000 patients per year and 40% don't start therapy, closing even half that gap with peer mentorship means 200 additional treatment starts. At $50,000 average annual therapy value, that's $10M in recovered revenue.
