# **Case Study: Supporting Retention in** **Trial Participants**

## **Background and Context**

A leading dermatology sponsor faced significant drop-off at high burden points in the patient treatment
journey. Although enrollment rates were strong, conversion to trial completion remained low.

## **Our Approach**


To address the identified participant challenges, the sponsor implemented PatientPartner’s peer-based
mentorship model to normalize the clinical trial experience and reduce hesitancy among trial participants.


With the launch of the PatientPartner program, mentors supported participants in two key ways:


1. **Direct Mentor Interactions** provided timely emotional support during periods of heightened
anxiety and burden, helping participants feel more confident and connected throughout the trial.


2. **Peer-based discussions** uncovered challenges that extended beyond the clinical protocol.
Patients described the emotional and social impact of visible treatment side effects - the
“looking worse before getting better” experience - as well as lifestyle restrictions such as
avoiding sun exposure or cosmetics, which reshaped daily routines and social confidence. They
also reported a sense of “study fatigue” driven by repeated questionnaires.


These experiential themes uncovered through peer conversations pointed to several actionable strategies
and tangible operational practices for improving retention and adherence in the trial, some examples
include:


**Normalize visible side effects.**


**Mentors** were able to provide anticipatory guidance through shared experience on
flare-ups and redness that may occur early in treatment, acting as ambassadors to
shared lived experience that reassured patients this phase is temporary.
Mentors recognized and celebrated milestones, sustained motivation by sharing
encouragement and practical tips.


**Ease lifestyle restrictions.**

**Sponsor** offered substitution strategies (trial-approved cosmetics, sun-safe
routines) to reduce the sense of loss, as well as educational materials for participants
to reframe the restrictions as temporary and purposeful, reinforcing their role in
protecting trial outcomes.


**Patient Experience Enhancements.**

**Sites**, when possible, offered waiting areas with additional privacy for patients
dealing with visible side effects, and were re-trained to proactively discuss common
lifestyle restrictions and provide trial-approved skincare kits (moisturizers,
sunscreen, and cosmetic alternatives) to ease discomfort and lifestyle restrictions.
